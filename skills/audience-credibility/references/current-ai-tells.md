# Current AI tells (2026)

A maintained list of design and copy patterns that have become recognisable "this was generated by an LLM" markers. These rot — re-audit periodically.

Each entry: what it is, why it became a tell, and what to do instead.

---

## Visual design

### Coloured top stripe / accent border on cards
A 2-6px coloured bar across the top of card components, usually using a different colour per card to indicate category. Became ubiquitous in AI-generated dashboards in 2024-25 because it's an "obvious" way to colour-code without needing real design judgement.

**Instead**: code differentiation through the content itself — coloured headline numbers, coloured icons inside the card, or a subtle left border. Or just trust the reader to read the card title.

### Diagonal gradient hero backgrounds
A purple-to-blue or pink-to-orange diagonal gradient across the hero section, often combined with a grid overlay and floating "code-like" decoration. Stripe-era SaaS aesthetic, now over-replicated.

**Instead**: a single solid brand colour, a subtle radial gradient that doesn't draw attention, or an actual photo/illustration relevant to the content.

### Two-tone gradient text on headlines
A headline where part of the wording transitions from one colour to another via `bg-clip-text`. Distinctive in mid-2020s SaaS marketing and now strongly tied to AI-generated landing pages.

**Instead**: solid colour, or a single highlighter-style background swatch behind specific words.

### Glassmorphism with backdrop-blur
Frosted-glass card effects with `backdrop-filter: blur()`, often layered over a gradient. Heavily overused.

**Instead**: solid backgrounds with shadow, or matte tinted surfaces.

### Three-card "feature" sections with icon + title + description
Three identical cards across, each with a Lucide-style line icon, a 2-3 word title, and a 1-line description. The "what does it do?" pattern.

**Instead**: vary the layout. Use one prose paragraph per feature, alternating image-and-text rows, or a single large hero showcase rather than parallel cards. If you must use cards, vary their size or content type.

### Lucide-icon-everywhere
Identical-style outline icons (Lucide, Heroicons) used reflexively next to every label, button, list item, and section heading.

**Instead**: use icons sparingly, where they genuinely aid scanning. Trust labels to do the work.

## Layout

### "Eyebrow → Heading → Lead" stack on every section
Every section opens with a small all-caps coloured label, then a large heading, then a single lead paragraph. Once distinctive; now reflexive.

**Instead**: vary section openings. Some can drop straight into prose. Others can lead with an image, a pull quote, or a question. Reserve the eyebrow stack for genuinely top-level sections.

### Pill-shaped buttons with chevron arrows
Rounded-full primary buttons with a "→" or chevron suffix. Heavy in AI-generated landing pages.

**Instead**: rectangular buttons with subtle radius, no arrow unless the button genuinely indicates progress to a next step.

### Centred everything
Everything centre-aligned: hero text, section text, button rows, image captions. Reads as "default LLM output".

**Instead**: left-align body text. Centre only where there's a specific reason (very short hero, single CTA).

### Equal-spacing grid uniformity
Every gap, padding, and margin set to the same value. Mathematically tidy but visually flat.

**Instead**: vary spacing meaningfully. Tight spacing for related items, loose spacing between sections. Look at any well-designed editorial layout for the pattern.

## Typography

### Heavy use of italics for emphasis in body copy
Italicising arbitrary phrases for emphasis or "feel". Reads as AI affectation in body text.

**Instead**: bold for genuine emphasis, otherwise let the writing carry itself.

### Em-dashes everywhere
Specifically: em-dashes used as a default sentence connector. Spotted by readers as a tell because LLMs over-favour them.

**Instead**: vary punctuation. Use full stops, semicolons, parentheses, or commas where appropriate. Don't avoid em-dashes entirely, but don't reach for them by default.

### Title-Case For Every Heading
Every word capitalised in section headings. Marketing-deck convention now over-applied.

**Instead**: sentence case for most contexts. Title case only where it fits the brand (newspapers, classical institutions).

## Copy

### "Reimagine / Empower / Transform / Unlock" verbs
Marketing verbs that pattern-match to AI-generated SaaS copy.

**Instead**: concrete verbs that describe what the thing actually does. "Manage" not "empower". "Show" not "reveal". "Combine" not "unify".

### "Powerful, intuitive, seamless" adjectives
Filler adjectives that sound positive but say nothing.

**Instead**: cut them, or replace with specifics. "Loads in under a second" beats "lightning-fast". "Two clicks to a result" beats "intuitive".

### "It's not just X — it's Y" rhetorical structure
A specific sentence pattern that LLMs over-produce.

**Instead**: just say what it is.

### "Whether you're X or Y, ..." inclusive opening
A sentence that opens by enumerating possible reader categories before getting to the point.

**Instead**: trust the reader to recognise themselves. Get to the point.

### Bullet points with emoji prefixes
A dot, arrow, or themed emoji at the start of bullet points. Reads as ChatGPT artefact.

**Instead**: standard markdown bullets. If you want visual interest, use a small icon as a separate column, not a leading character in the text.

### "Let's dive into..." / "Let's unpack..." transitions
Conversational scene-setting phrases between sections.

**Instead**: cut them. The next section's heading already tells the reader what's coming.

### Tricolons everywhere
Every list and every sentence-of-three structured as "X, Y, and Z" — even when there are only two real things and the third is filler.

**Instead**: use the number of items the content actually needs. Two is fine. Four is fine.

## Structure

### Conclusion that summarises what was just said
A "Summary" or "Conclusion" section at the end of a report that restates the headline finding. Common in AI-written reports because the model is trained on essay structure.

**Instead**: end on the most useful next-action or open question. Trust the reader to remember the headline.

### Excessive section headers
Subheadings every 2-3 paragraphs, breaking up prose that doesn't need it.

**Instead**: only use a heading when there's a genuine topic shift. Continuous prose is fine for related ideas.

### "Here's what we'll cover" preamble
An introductory paragraph that previews the structure of the document.

**Instead**: just start. The reader can see the headings.

### Mandatory "Key Takeaways" callout box
A coloured box at the top or bottom restating 3-4 bullet points from the body.

**Instead**: write the body to be scannable in the first place. If a takeaway box is genuinely useful, fine, but don't reflexively add one.

## How to update this list

Add a new entry when:
- A pattern is appearing in 3+ unrelated AI-generated outputs you've seen recently
- The pattern carries a "this is AI" connotation for an attentive audience, even if it was once neutral

Remove an entry when:
- The pattern has been adopted widely enough by hand-built work that it no longer reads as AI-specific
- A new replacement pattern has emerged for the same need

Date-stamp the bottom of the file so the next reader knows how stale it is.
